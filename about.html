Masindi Nafisa Mbolekwa (b.2002) is a visual artist from Johannesburg. Mbolekwa's practice is an engagement with the relationship between the body and it's movement through exterior space, and the reflection of space in the interior. Drawing from a Xhosa cosmological understanding of space, where land is interwoven within memory, identity and language, and movement through it is ritual and a critical aspect of self, Mbolekwa's painting taps into a mytho-poetic realm where the body is in constant negotiation with it's surroundings or lack thereof. In this work, the body’s movements are a reflection of the constant metaphysical re-affirmation of black being-in-the-world, and the spaces and non-spaces through which it moves are agentic forces within themselves.


Mbolekwa holds an Honours in Fine Art from the University of The Witwatersrand (2023). His first Solo presentation “Feast of Flesh and Prayer” was held at Ellis House Gallery in May 2025. He has participated in various group exhibitions including ‘Screen-In-Time’ (2022) at The Point of Order, ‘Siyabuka’ (2023) at Asisebenze Art Atelier, ‘Crossroads: Tsila Tse Pedi’ (2024) at the Viewing Room Gallery, ‘Figurative Frontiers’ (2024) at RMB Latitudes with Origin Art, Tswela Pele’ (2025) with Kalashnikovv Gallery and Symptom: Invisible Forces (2025) with Bode Gallery. Mbolekwa has works in the Nandos UK Art Collection and Moleskine Foundation Collection.


Statement c.2.26


Throughout this work rests an unsettled and embodied desire to through being, belong, and to belong in being.
Worked with ritual care into every expression and void in these paintings is my experience of the constant reaffirmation of self and negotiation of home, of the body and its relation to space, and its ability to be both held by and sundered at the intersections of spatial, historical and cultural identity. In the union of painterly formalism, earth-based material and abstraction onto surface, there is an attempt to divorce home – in all its complexities and function within the Xhosa culture – from place, to which it is bound through the epistemic violences of empire and hegemony.


Drawing from the treacherous ontological grounds of blackness in the contemporary postcolony, home and belonging are interrogated here from my perspective as a Johannesburg born and resident black, with a faraway home in the Eastern Cape village of Macacuma. Birthed from this troubled semantics of place as home, is a grappling with the self as a situated being, facing the in-hospitability of a historic-colonial center and the wide chasm of a distant and unfamiliar home. This work is an attempt to unground being from place, not only for the sake of desire, but as an act of necessity, for place and its politic have so vehemently rejected this body.


On the canvas a poetry of the ineffable occurs. An acknowledgement of the vastness, the nihility, and the pessimism which surrounds black ontological homelessness becomes the very reason that the work must be made, an anti-empiricist pushback based on the knowledge of the spirit. In the logical acknowledgement of an Afropessimist reality, this work is an attempt at a spiritual circumvention of logic itself, for it was western logic that wrought upon blackness, the malady of blackness. In this attempt, there is a radical practice of existential hope. In the attempt there hides an amen shot through the day into the night-world of ideas, into the 2-dimensional, into the meta-reality.
